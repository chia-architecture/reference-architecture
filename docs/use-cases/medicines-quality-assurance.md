# Medicines quality assurance
Caribbean Member States rely on regional and national mechanisms to ensure that medicines available to patients are safe, effective, and of high quality. Within CARPHA, the Caribbean Regulatory System (CRS) reviews medicines, vaccines, and other health products and makes recommendations to CARICOM Member States for market authorization or import approval. These assessments are often based on reliance on trusted sources such as the World Health Organization (WHO) prequalification programme or recognised regulatory authorities.

Once a product is recommended, the decision and associated product information—such as manufacturer, formulation, and approval status—are recorded in the CRS Registry, which acts as a regional product catalogue. National authorities use this information to support local regulatory decisions and procurement. In parallel, the CARPHA Medicines Quality Control and Surveillance Department (MQCSD) performs laboratory testing on selected products, such as during post-market surveillance or in response to suspected quality issues. The results of these tests provide additional evidence on product quality and may inform regulatory actions.

This use case illustrates how a cross-border healthcare architecture can support medicines governance by linking regulatory assessment, laboratory validation, and a shared product catalogue. It enables consistent identification of medicines, improves transparency of quality status across countries, and supports coordinated regulatory and public health action at regional level.

## Challenge today
- Fragmented information on medicines across countries and organisations
- Limited visibility of regulatory decisions and product status across the region
- Inconsistent identification of medicines, manufacturers, and batches
- Delays in sharing laboratory results and regulatory updates
- Limited integration between regulatory, laboratory, and procurement systems

## Desired situation
- A shared and trusted product catalogue (CRS Registry) containing medicines and their regulatory status
- Regulatory recommendations and approvals are accessible across countries
- Laboratory testing results are linked to specific products and batches
- Procurement and healthcare providers can access up-to-date product information
- Coordinated regional response to quality issues or recalls

## Required capabilities
- Unique identification of medicines, manufacturers, and batches
- A shared product catalogue / medicine registry accessible across countries
- Secure exchange of regulatory decisions and laboratory results
- Standardised data formats for medicines and quality information
- Integration between regulatory, laboratory, and supply chain systems
- Traceability of product lifecycle, from approval to post-market surveillance

## Related building blocks
- Product Catalogue / Medicine Registry (CRS Registry)
- Product / Batch Identity
- Health Data Exchange
- Laboratory Information Exchange
- Terminology & Semantics
- Audit & Logging

## Impact
- Improved safety and quality of medicines across the region
- Faster detection and response to substandard or counterfeit medicines
- More efficient and aligned regulatory decision-making
- Increased transparency and trust in the medicines supply chain
- Better use of regional expertise and reliance mechanisms
